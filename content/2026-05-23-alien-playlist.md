### The Final Task

In another life, things didn't work out so well for you. Although ultimately harmless, a youthful indiscretion that would become known as "The Incident" among your town would haunt your career trajectory for the rest of your life. You somehow muddled through school, ultimately ending up working a dead-end job at NASA, where you served as a low-level technician on the graveyard shift at the Emergency Rocket Facility. 

Heavily influenced by a series of popular films at the time, the Emergency Rocket Facility was built in the late 1990s, with the purpose of maintaining one rocketship that was always ready to launch a crew of sexy, ragtag misfits who could deal with any space emergencies that arose. After decades of never being needed, Congress began to see the ERF as a wasteful extravagance, and significantly reduced its budget. 

Being the cheapest to employ, you survived the mass layoff that ensued, and very quickly discovered that promotions were now easy to come by. Everyone who could manage to get a job elsewhere jumped at the opportunity to flee that dying ship, offering a series of stepping stones that allowed you to rise to the rank of Chief Administrator (Graveyard Shift) in less than two years.

It's 3 AM. You stare out at the majestic rocket, illuminated by a series of dazzling, constantly moving spotlights. Linda called out sick today, leaving you as the sole person at the facility. Other than that, it's a night like any other night.

The red phone rings. But...the red phone, it...never rings? Why is it ringing? Suddenly, you realize the significance of this phone call. You pick up. The President's Chief of Staff informs you of the terrible news: a planetoid moving at a significant fraction of the speed of light is headed directly towards Earth. In ten minutes, Earth will *literally* be vaporized. No evidence that there was ever life here will remain.

You're given one final task: launch something - anything - into space, *right now*, so that if by some miracle an alien civilization finds it, they can at least know something of us. There's no time to ship anything to the ERF. There's no time to take a vote or assemble a committee. You hang up, and quickly look around. Nothing but filing cabinets and old computers from the 90s.

Then you see it: a CD-R. A writeable optical disk, capable of storing 13 songs. Once written, it can be neither rewritten nor deleted. You quickly take out your phone and access your enormous library of pirated music. Did I mention the scandal was about how you operated the world's then-largest music piracy ring, and that you never really gave up the life, and just went deeper underground to evade the authorities? In any event, you have access to the entire catalog of recorded audio produced by humanity at your fingertips, in the highest quality available.

You have just a few minutes to assemble the last testament to humanity's existence. What would the optimal playlist be? If you choose to go through this exercise, send me yours: [jim@rybarski.com](mailto:jim@rybarski.com)

### FAQ

Q: Am I supposed to -
A: No. There's no *supposed to*. There's no accountability. You and every human will be dead in ten minutes. No one can judge your choices, or offer their assistance. There's no time for someone to tell you what to value or how to choose. In fact, you don't even need to launch anything. You can choose to be completely derelict in your duties if you want.

Q: Will the aliens know how to decode the CD and listen to it?
A: We'll assume they can figure it out and that their auditory organs and qualia operate identically to our own. They also live in an atmosphere with the same pressure and composition as Earth's. We never learned this, of course, or the reason why this in particular is bizarrely necessary for technological life to emerge on a planet. Such a shame we had to go so soon.

Q: Does it have to be music?
A: If it's recorded audio, or the audio track of any kind of video, it's available (even non-digitized media are available - you really were a great pirate). You can also record anything you like in five minutes (the other five minutes are needed to write the disk, load the cargo and launch the rocket).

Q: Don't CD-Rs degrade after a few decades, and probably faster when exposed to intense cosmic radiation?
A: It's a special NASA CD-R that will last for hundreds of billions of years.
